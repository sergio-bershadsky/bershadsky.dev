# Your First Knowledge Entry

> Stop reading about second brains. Let's build something in the next 10 minutes.

You've read about pillars and structures. Now let's get your hands dirty with a real knowledge entry — and experience the moment when your AI actually remembers something between sessions.

## The Goal

Here's what we're doing today:
- Create one piece of lasting knowledge
- Watch Claude remember it in future sessions
- Feel the satisfaction of a system that works

**Time commitment:** 10 minutes of setup, a lifetime of payoff. Or at least until the next framework rewrite.

**What you'll need:**
- The setup from Part 2 (or just a markdown file if you're impatient)
- Something you've explained more than twice
- Coffee optional, patience recommended

## Choosing Your First Entry

Not all knowledge is created equal. For your first entry, we want something that hits the sweet spot.

```
GOOD FIRST ENTRIES                 SKIP FOR NOW
─────────────────                  ─────────────
Things you explain repeatedly      Complex architecture docs
Project setup steps                Comprehensive API reference
Team conventions                   Everything you know
Common commands                    Your life story
```

**Examples that work well:**
- "How to set up the development environment"
- "Our naming conventions"
- "Why we use [specific tool]"
- "Common commands I always forget"

The rule is simple: **if you've explained something more than twice, it deserves a document.** Your future self (and future teammates) will thank you.

> Pick something boring. Seriously. Your first knowledge entry shouldn't be a masterpiece — it should be useful. Save the creative writing for your novel.

## Writing It Down

Here's the process:

```
Step 1: Tell Claude what you know
        ↓
Step 2: Let Claude organize it
        ↓
Step 3: Review and refine
        ↓
Step 4: Save to your knowledge base
```

**Sample conversation:**

```
You:    "I need to document how to set up our dev environment.
         Here's what I usually tell people: [your explanation]"

Claude: "Let me organize that into a structured guide..."
        [Returns formatted markdown]

You:    "Add the part about the environment variables"

Claude: [Updates the document]

You:    "Perfect. Save this to docs/guides/dev-setup.md"
```

**Key insight:** You don't have to write perfectly. You just have to capture the knowledge. Claude helps with structure; you provide the substance.

**What your knowledge entry might look like:**

A simple setup guide with prerequisites, step-by-step instructions, and common troubleshooting tips. Nothing fancy — just the stuff you'd tell someone in person, organized in a way that's easy to follow.

## The Magic Moment

Now for the payoff. Start a **new** Claude session (this is important — we want to prove it remembers):

```

Claude: "Based on your documentation, here are the steps..."

```

**What just happened:**

## Building the Habit

When should you document? Here's a quick decision matrix:

```
DOCUMENT                     JUST DO IT
────────                     ──────────
Explained twice              One-off tasks
Core to the project          Trivial fixes
Others will need this        Personal preferences
Complex/easy to forget       Obvious stuff
```

**The "explain once, remember forever" mindset:** Every time you find yourself explaining something, ask: "Will I explain this again?" If yes, take 5 minutes to document it. You're not creating bureaucracy — you're buying back future time.

**Signs you need a knowledge entry:**
- You've Googled the same thing three times
- You've said "let me walk you through this" more than once
- You've forgotten your own clever solution
- Someone asked and you couldn't remember the details

> Documentation is like flossing. Nobody enjoys it in the moment, but future you will have much better teeth. Or... fewer production incidents. The metaphor kind of falls apart, but you get the idea.
