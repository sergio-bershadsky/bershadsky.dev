## The Greenfield Opportunity

When Moxy reached out to me in 2020, they had an ambitious vision but no infrastructure. Zero. Nada. Just a brilliant idea for a digital sports and entertainment platform and a team of talented developers who needed someone to build the foundation they'd develop on.

Starting from scratch is both terrifying and exhilarating. There's no legacy baggage to deal with, no "that's how we've always done it" conversations. But there's also no safety net—every architectural decision you make will echo through the platform's future.

The challenge was clear: build infrastructure that could handle the unpredictable traffic patterns of live sports events while keeping costs manageable for a startup that was still finding its footing.

## Understanding the Beast

Sports entertainment has unique infrastructure requirements. Traffic isn't predictable like an e-commerce site. It spikes dramatically during live events—sometimes going from hundreds to tens of thousands of concurrent users in minutes—then drops back to baseline.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  MOXY TRAFFIC // PATTERN ANALYSIS               ARCHITECTURE │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  CHALLENGE: UNPREDICTABLE SPIKES                             │
│  ├── Pre-event: ~500 users baseline                          │
│  ├── Event start: 10,000+ concurrent users (20x spike)       │
│  ├── Peak moments: Critical plays = additional 50% surge     │
│  └── Post-event: Rapid decline to baseline                   │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  REQUIREMENTS DERIVED:                                        │
│  ├── Auto-scaling: Must handle 20x traffic in < 2 minutes    │
│  ├── Latency: < 200ms globally for real-time experience      │
│  ├── Availability: 99.99% during live events (zero downtime) │
│  └── Cost: Scale down aggressively when traffic drops        │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Traditional infrastructure would either be massively over-provisioned (expensive) or unable to handle spikes (broken user experience). We needed something smarter.

## The Architecture Blueprint

I designed a cloud-native architecture on AWS that embraced the ephemeral nature of modern infrastructure. Nothing was permanent. Everything was code. Every component could scale independently.

The key design decisions:

**Kubernetes from Day One.** Some startups start with simpler orchestration and migrate later. I argued for Kubernetes immediately. Yes, it has a learning curve. But the scaling capabilities and ecosystem were exactly what Moxy needed for unpredictable traffic patterns.

**Infrastructure as Code with Terraform.** Every resource—from VPCs to IAM policies—was defined in Terraform. This meant we could spin up identical environments for development, staging, and production. It also meant disaster recovery was a matter of running `terraform apply` in a new region.

**Multi-Layered Caching.** CloudFront for edge caching, ElastiCache for application-level caching. Content that didn't change frequently was served from cache, reducing load on the application servers and keeping latency low globally.

The pipeline reduced deployment times by 75% compared to manual processes. More importantly, it gave developers confidence. They could push code knowing that if something was wrong, the pipeline would catch it before it reached users.

## Handling the First Live Event

The real test came during Moxy's first major live event. We'd done load testing, stress testing, chaos engineering. But nothing prepares you like real users doing unpredictable things.

The traffic started building an hour before the event. By the time it started, we had 8,000 concurrent users—and climbing. I watched the Kubernetes autoscaler spin up new pods. CloudWatch metrics showed everything in the green. The CDN was absorbing most of the static content requests.

Then the peak moment hit. A critical play that everyone wanted to see simultaneously. Traffic spiked another 25% in thirty seconds.

The infrastructure held. Latency stayed under 200ms. Not a single error in the logs.

That was the moment I knew the architecture was right.

## What I Learned

Every project teaches you something. Moxy reinforced a belief I'd developed over years of consulting: the best time to make architectural decisions is at the beginning, when the cost of change is lowest.

Startups often want to move fast and fix things later. But "later" has a cost. Technical debt accumulates interest. The infrastructure decisions you make in month one will either accelerate or constrain you in year three.

Moxy trusted me to make those early decisions carefully. That trust paid off with a platform that could scale with their growth rather than becoming a bottleneck to it.
