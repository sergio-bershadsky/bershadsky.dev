In 2015, I joined a project that would become one of the most rewarding challenges of my career. A small news portal about Moscow's healthcare digitization was hemorrhaging money, averaging just 1,000-2,000 visitors per month. The owner was ready to pull the plug.

Three years later, by 2018, that same portal had transformed into the **largest medical appointment booking mobile application in the world**, according to McKinsey's Smart Cities 2018 report. We achieved over 1 million monthly active users, 1.6 million app installations, and processed 2 million doctor appointments every single month.

This is the story of how we did it.

## The Starting Point: A System Without Users

When I came on board in early 2015, Moscow had already implemented a Hospital Information System (HIS) across all state clinics. The system worked well for automating doctor workflows — scheduling, patient records, prescription management — but there was a fundamental problem: getting citizens to actually use online booking.

The city government had invested heavily in digitization. They'd opened an API for medical appointment booking, initially exclusive to the state-run portal. Every clinic in Moscow was connected. The infrastructure was solid. But after a full year of operation, online bookings had plateaued at just 6% of total appointments. 

The remaining 94% were still happening through phone calls and in-person visits. This was expensive for the city (call center staff, physical reception desks, administrative overhead) and inconvenient for patients (long hold times, limited availability, wasted trips).

The state portal controlled 93% of whatever online bookings existed. We were a tiny fish in a small pond, running a news website about healthcare that nobody visited.

## The Dire Situation

The news portal had been launched to promote the HIS implementation — essentially a government PR project that had outlived its usefulness. The commercial operations were minimal, and the content wasn't driving engagement. Traffic had stagnated at 1,000-2,000 monthly visitors, barely enough to justify the hosting costs.

The owner was ready to shut everything down. The team was small, the resources were limited, and there was no clear path to profitability. In a final attempt to salvage something from the project, the owner proposed transferring product management to our team.

We received full carte blanche to do whatever we wanted, with one critical constraint: the city leadership had regulatory restrictions we had to work within. We couldn't bypass their systems or compete directly with government infrastructure.

## The Pivot Decision: Kill the News, Build a Service

After two weeks of intensive analysis — reviewing traffic patterns, user behavior, competitive landscape, and the owner's remaining resources — we made a radical decision: **kill the news content entirely**.

Every ruble previously spent on creating articles, hiring writers, and managing content would be redirected to building a service. No more editorial calendar. No more content strategy. Just product development.

But what service exactly? The state portal already existed and had 93% market share. We couldn't simply clone it — that would be pointless and potentially problematic with regulators. We needed to find a wedge, a niche that the government portal wasn't serving well.

## Finding Our Wedge: The Friction Problem

We conducted user research over several weeks, talking to dozens of Moscow residents about their healthcare booking experiences. What we discovered was revealing.

The state portal required mandatory registration before booking. You needed to create an account, verify your identity through a government authentication system, and navigate a complex interface designed by bureaucrats rather than UX designers.

For people who rarely visited doctors — the "healthy skeptics" who only needed an appointment once or twice a year — this was a massive barrier. They didn't want to create another account. They didn't want to remember another password. They didn't want to deal with government authentication flows.

They just wanted to see a doctor.

Our answer: **one-click booking without registration**.

It sounds simple now, but in 2015, this was genuinely innovative for government-connected services. We stripped away every unnecessary step. No account creation. No email verification. No identity confirmation upfront. Just pick your doctor, pick your time, confirm with your phone number, done.

The phone number served as a lightweight identifier — enough for the clinic to contact the patient and confirm the appointment, without the friction of full registration.

## The Mobile-First Bet

While our competitors focused on desktop web experiences, we made another strategic bet that proved crucial: **mobile would win**.

Moscow's smartphone penetration was exploding. People were checking their phones on the metro, during lunch breaks, in waiting rooms. The state portal's desktop-first design felt clunky on mobile browsers. We saw an opportunity.

We launched three products in rapid succession throughout 2016:

1. **A completely redesigned web portal** focused on booking, not news. Clean interface, fast load times, prominent search for doctors and clinics.

2. **An adaptive mobile web version** optimized for smartphone browsers. Same functionality as desktop, but designed thumb-first.

3. **Native mobile applications for iOS and Android**. Not just wrapped websites, but truly native experiences built with ReactNative.

The mobile apps became our secret weapon. While competitors treated mobile as an afterthought — a shrunken version of their desktop site — we built ours with mobile-first principles. Push notifications reminded patients of upcoming appointments. The booking flow was optimized for one-handed use. Favorites let you quickly rebook with preferred doctors.

## The Technical Architecture

Building a system that could handle millions of appointments required serious engineering. As a senior developer on the team, I was deeply involved in the technical decisions that would determine whether we'd scale or fail.

**Frontend Stack:**
- ReactJS for the web portal — component-based architecture that let us iterate quickly
- ReactNative for iOS and Android apps — shared codebase, native performance

**Backend Infrastructure:**
- Python powering our API services — Django for rapid development, Celery for background tasks
- Docker containers for consistent deployments across environments
- Jenkins for continuous integration — automated testing on every commit
- Consul for service discovery — as we added more microservices, we needed them to find each other
- Tyk as our API gateway — rate limiting, authentication, analytics in one place

**Monitoring & Analytics:**
- ELK stack (Elasticsearch, Logstash, Kibana) for log analysis and debugging
- Zabbix for infrastructure monitoring — CPU, memory, disk, network across all servers
- Sentry for error tracking — real-time alerts when something broke
- Google Analytics for user behavior insights — funnel analysis, conversion optimization

The architecture needed to be resilient. When you're handling 2 million medical appointments per month, downtime isn't just an inconvenience — it's people missing their healthcare. We designed for redundancy at every layer.

## Scaling Challenges: Growing Pains

Growth brought its own problems. Issues we never anticipated during initial development became critical at scale.

**Database Performance:** Our appointment matching algorithm had to search through thousands of available slots across hundreds of clinics in real-time. Early on, queries took 2-3 seconds. Users were bouncing. We implemented aggressive caching with Redis, query optimization with proper indexes, and eventually sharded our appointment data by district. Response times dropped below 200ms.

**API Rate Limiting:** The city's HIS API had rate limits we had to respect. During peak hours — Monday mornings were brutal — we'd hit these limits and bookings would fail. We built a sophisticated queuing system that batched requests intelligently, prioritizing time-sensitive operations and gracefully degrading when the upstream system was overwhelmed.

**Mobile App Updates:** With over a million installations by late 2017, we couldn't push breaking changes without careful migration strategies. A bad update could strand users on broken versions. We implemented feature flags that let us gradually roll out changes to user segments — 1%, then 5%, then 25%, then 100%. If something broke, we could roll back instantly.

**Peak Load Management:** Doctor appointment bookings aren't evenly distributed. Monday mornings saw 5-10x normal traffic. We implemented auto-scaling on our Kubernetes clusters, pre-warming caches before predicted peaks, and circuit breakers to prevent cascade failures.

## Lessons Learned: What This Project Taught Me

**1. Find the friction, remove it ruthlessly.** The state portal made registration mandatory because "that's how things are done." We questioned that assumption and won. Every step you add to a user flow is a percentage of users you'll lose.

**2. Mobile isn't optional.** In 2015-2016, many teams still treated mobile apps as nice-to-haves. Our mobile-first approach captured users our competitors couldn't reach. Today this seems obvious; back then it was a bet.

**3. Speed is a feature.** Our obsession with sub-200ms response times wasn't about technical bragging rights. It was about respecting users' time and building trust. Fast apps feel professional. Slow apps feel unreliable.

**4. Data drives decisions.** Every change we made was measured. When we saw one-click booking performing 3x better than registered booking, we doubled down. When we saw mobile traffic exceeding desktop, we shifted resources. Intuition gets you started; data keeps you on course.

**5. Government partnerships require patience.** Working within city restrictions taught us to be creative within constraints rather than fighting against them. We couldn't change the rules, but we could find opportunities the rules didn't cover.

**6. Small teams can beat giants.** The state portal had government resources, official branding, and years of head start. We had a handful of engineers and no marketing budget. But we were faster, more user-focused, and more willing to experiment.

## The Bigger Picture

This project taught me that sometimes the biggest opportunities hide inside dying products. The news portal everyone wanted to shut down contained a kernel of potential that just needed the right vision to unlock.

We didn't have the state's resources or brand recognition. We didn't have a marketing department or a PR team. We had a small team, a willingness to question assumptions, and the technical skills to execute quickly.

From 2015 to 2018, we went from a failing news site to a platform processing 2 million monthly appointments. Sometimes that's what it takes: seeing potential where others see failure, and having the courage to pivot completely.

Looking back, this project shaped how I think about product development, technical architecture, and user experience. The lessons I learned scaling UMIAS have influenced every project I've worked on since.

Sometimes, all a dying portal needs is permission to become something else entirely.
