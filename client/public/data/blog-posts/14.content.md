# Core Building Blocks

> The three pillars that make a second brain actually work: current knowledge, historical record, and decision archive.

**Reading time:** 7-10 minutes | **Audience:** Non-Technical

---

## The Opening

Every good system has three things: what you know now, what you've discussed, and what you've decided. Let's build all three.

Most knowledge systems fail because they're just dumping grounds. Everything goes in, nothing comes out useful. You create a folder called "Notes" and six months later it's a graveyard of half-finished thoughts and documents named "final_v3_ACTUALLY_FINAL.docx".

A second brain is different. It's organized by purpose, not just by date. It knows the difference between "how we do things" and "why we do things that way." And crucially, it can answer both questions without you digging through seventeen folders.

Today we're going to meet the three pillars that make this work.

---

## The Three Pillars

Your second brain rests on three distinct foundations. Each one answers a different type of question, and together they give you something no single note-taking app ever could: actual understanding.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              YOUR SECOND BRAIN                   │
├─────────────────┬─────────────────┬─────────────┤
│  CURRENT        │  HISTORICAL     │  DECISIONS  │
│  KNOWLEDGE      │  RECORD         │  ARCHIVE    │
│                 │                 │             │
│  "How we do     │  "What we       │  "Why we    │
│   things now"   │   discussed"    │   chose"    │
│                 │                 │             │
│  • Guides       │  • Discussions  │  • Choices  │
│  • How-tos      │  • Meetings     │  • Rejected │
│  • References   │  • Context      │    options  │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────┴─────────────┘
```

Here's the key insight: these three pillars answer fundamentally different questions.

**"How?"** lives in current knowledge. It's your instruction manual.

**"What happened?"** lives in history. It's your memory.

**"Why?"** lives in decisions. It's your wisdom.

When someone asks "why do we use Redis for caching?" you need all three. Knowledge tells them how to set it up. History tells them about the performance problems that led to the discussion. Decisions tell them why Redis beat the alternatives.

Miss any pillar and you're missing context. And missing context is how you end up having the same conversation every three months.

---

## Pillar One: Current Knowledge (The Living Docs)

Current knowledge is the "how we do things now" layer. It's your living documentation — guides, best practices, reference materials, and procedures that evolve as you learn.

The key word here is *living*. Unlike a wiki page someone wrote in 2019 and never touched again, current knowledge reflects reality. When you discover a better way, you update it. When something becomes obsolete, you remove it.

```
CURRENT KNOWLEDGE
─────────────────
• Changes frequently
• Reflects latest understanding
• Answers "how do I...?"
• Written for reuse

Examples:
- Setup guides
- Best practices
- Reference materials
- Standard procedures
```

Think of current knowledge as the instruction manual you wish your project came with. The one that actually matches reality, not the optimistic version written before anyone used the thing.

**Why version control matters:** Every change is saved. You can see what something looked like last month, last year, or before that "helpful" update that broke everything. Git does this automatically — we'll set that up shortly.

The beauty of living docs is that they compound. Write a setup guide once, update it occasionally, and save yourself from explaining the same thing fifty times. Your future self (and your teammates) will thank you.

---

## Pillar Two: Historical Record (The Memory)

If current knowledge is your instruction manual, historical record is your memory. It's the archive of discussions, meetings, and conversations that shaped where you are now.

Historical records are different from current knowledge in one crucial way: they rarely change after creation. A meeting summary from last Tuesday is what it is. You're not going to "update" it when circumstances change — you'll create a new discussion instead.

```
HISTORICAL RECORD
─────────────────
• Rarely changes after creation
• Captures moment in time
• Answers "what did we discuss?"
• Written for context

Examples:
- Team discussions
- Meeting notes
- Planning sessions
- Troubleshooting sessions
```

Here's why this matters: history isn't just what happened — it's why you understand where you are now. Skip tracking history and you'll constantly be asking "wait, why did we do it this way?"

**The new team member test:** Someone joins your project. Without a historical record, they need weeks of painful interviews and context-building conversations. "Oh, you had to be there for that decision." "Ask Sarah, she remembers."

With a historical record, they read the discussions. "Oh, that's why the architecture looks like that. Makes sense now." Two hours instead of two weeks.

Your AI assistant especially benefits from this. Give Claude access to your discussion history and suddenly it can answer "what did we talk about regarding the API redesign?" with actual context, not a confused stare.

## Pillar Three: Decision Archive (The Wisdom)

The third pillar is where the real magic happens. Decisions are the "why we chose" layer — they document what you decided, why you decided it, and crucially, what you rejected.

You might have heard these called "Architectural Decision Records" or ADRs. Sounds fancy. Don't worry — it's just organized choices. "Dear Diary, we picked X because..." with better formatting.

Here's what a good decision looks like:

```
┌───────────────────────────────────────┐
│  DECISION: Which Database to Use      │
├───────────────────────────────────────┤
│  STATUS: Implemented                  │
│                                       │
│  CONTEXT:                             │
│  We needed a database that handles    │
│  high read volume with low latency    │
│                                       │
│  OPTIONS CONSIDERED:                  │
│  1. PostgreSQL (chosen) — fits our    │
│     scale and team expertise          │
│  2. MongoDB (rejected) — schema       │
│     flexibility not needed            │
│  3. Build custom (rejected) — would   │
│     take 6 months we don't have       │
│                                       │
│  CONSEQUENCES:                        │
│  Need to learn advanced indexing,     │
│  can't easily do document-style data  │
└───────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Why rejected options matter:** Six months from now, someone will suggest MongoDB. Instead of having the whole discussion again, you point to the decision record: "We considered that. Here's why we didn't go that way." Conversation over.

Decisions also have a lifecycle:

```
Proposed → Accepted → Implemented
              ↓
           Rejected (with reason)
```

Some decisions are still being discussed (proposed). Some were accepted but not yet implemented. Some were rejected — and that's valuable too, because it prevents relitigating the same options.

The decision archive is honestly the highest-leverage part of your second brain. It prevents circular conversations, onboards new people faster, and gives your AI the "why" behind everything.

## How The Pillars Connect

These three pillars don't exist in isolation. They flow into each other naturally:

```
DISCUSSION ──────▶ DECISION ──────▶ KNOWLEDGE
    │                  │                │
"We should use        "We decided      "Here's how
 a caching layer"      to use Redis"    to set it up"
```

Here's a real example of how this works:

1. **Historical Record:** Team discusses performance problems. "The API is slow. Users are complaining. We need to do something about caching."

2. **Decision Archive:** You evaluate options — Redis, Memcached, building something custom. You choose Redis because it fits your scale and has better persistence. You document why you rejected the others.

3. **Current Knowledge:** Someone writes a guide on setting up Redis in your environment. How to configure it, common gotchas, debugging tips.

Now everything links together:
- Decisions reference the discussions that led to them
- Knowledge links to decisions that justify the approach
- Everything is searchable, everything connects

**The payoff:** When someone asks "why Redis?" you have three layers of answer:
- Knowledge: "Here's how we use it"
- Decision: "Here's why we chose it"
- History: "Here's the discussion that led there"

That's not just documentation. That's institutional memory. And it's the difference between a project that can scale with new people and one that falls apart when the original team moves on.

## Setting Up Your Portal

Enough theory. Let's get practical. Here's how to set up the foundation for your second brain in about five minutes.

```bash
# One-time setup
npm create vitepress@latest my-second-brain
cd my-second-brain
npm install
npm run dev
```

That gives you a fast, searchable documentation site running locally. But the magic is in how you organize it.

Here's the folder structure that implements our three pillars:

```
my-second-brain/
├── docs/
│   ├── guides/           # Current knowledge
│   │   └── getting-started.md
│   ├── discussions/      # Historical record
│   │   └── TEMPLATE.md
│   └── decisions/        # Decision archive
│       └── TEMPLATE.md
└── package.json
```

Three folders. Three purposes. That's it.

Don't worry about filling all these folders yet. An empty structure is still a structure. In the next article, we'll create your first real knowledge entry and watch your second brain actually remember something.

## Key Takeaway

> Structure isn't bureaucracy — it's kindness to your future self.

When you organize knowledge by purpose — what you know, what you discussed, why you decided — you're not creating overhead. You're creating leverage. Every entry makes the next question easier to answer.

Your AI assistant can't help you remember if there's nothing to remember. These three pillars give it (and you) something to work with.
