## The Growing Pains of Success

WellBe had built something special—a mental wellness platform offering personalized meditation, psychology content, and wellbeing recommendations that users genuinely loved. The problem wasn't the product; it was the foundation it was built on.

When I joined the team in 2023, WellBe was experiencing the classic symptoms of early-stage technical decisions catching up with growth. Firebase, which had enabled rapid initial development, was now becoming a constraint rather than an enabler.

The team had ambitious plans for subscription features, personalized notifications, and content delivery—but every new feature felt like fighting against the architecture rather than building with it.

## The Firebase Ceiling

Each had different webhook formats, event types, and edge cases. Without a unified system, the team was constantly firefighting subscription issues.

## The New Architecture

I designed a modern, microservices-based architecture using **Django with Strawberry GraphQL**. Strawberry's Python-first, type-safe approach to GraphQL was perfect for our team—it integrated seamlessly with Django's ORM and gave us compile-time validation of our API schemas. This would give WellBe the flexibility to iterate quickly while maintaining reliability at scale.

The key architectural decisions:

**GraphQL as the API Layer.** Instead of REST endpoints that would require app updates for schema changes, GraphQL gave the mobile team flexibility. New fields could be added server-side, and the Flutter app could query exactly what it needed.

**Microservices for Domain Separation.** Subscriptions, notifications, users, and analytics each became independent services. This meant the subscription team could deploy payment fixes without touching the notification system.

**Heroku for Simplicity.** For a team focused on product rather than infrastructure, Heroku provided the right balance of power and simplicity. Pipelines for staging and production, easy scaling, and managed PostgreSQL kept operational overhead low.

**Unified Webhook Handler.** A dedicated service normalized events from App Store, Google Play, and Yukassa into a consistent format. The subscription service didn't need to know which payment provider originated an event.

## The Notification Revolution

The old notification system was a constant source of frustration. Messages were unreliable, scheduling was rigid, and there was no visibility into delivery status.

I designed a centralized notification service that supported both automated triggers and manual campaigns. The system could route messages based on user preferences, timezone, and engagement patterns. Delivery tracking let the product team understand what was actually reaching users.

Push notification reliability jumped from sporadic to consistent. The marketing team could now run targeted campaigns without engineering involvement.

## The Subscription Lifecycle

This was the crown jewel of the migration. Every subscription event—creation, renewal, cancellation, grace period, billing retry, expiration—was now tracked and actionable.

The webhook handler processed events from all three payment platforms and normalized them into a consistent lifecycle model. When a subscription entered a grace period, the system could automatically send retention notifications. When a payment failed, support had visibility into the issue before the user complained.

Most importantly, the team could now answer questions like "how many users churned last month after their trial ended?" without manual data extraction.

## What Made This Work

Mental health apps require particular attention to reliability. Users depending on meditation reminders or therapy session notifications need those messages to arrive. The new architecture delivered that reliability while also enabling the rapid iteration a startup needs.

The key was respecting what WellBe had already built while providing a clear migration path. We didn't rewrite everything at once—we migrated incrementally, service by service, always maintaining a working product.
